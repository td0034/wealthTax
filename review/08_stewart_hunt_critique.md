# Stewart/Hunt critique of the cap paper

Adversarial critique simulated from two specific personas: Rory Stewart
(centrist liberal-conservative, ex-DfID, deeply attentive to
institutional capacity and historical precedent) and Jeremy Hunt
(ex-Chancellor, technocratic Conservative, business-confidence and
tax-competitiveness lens).

Written to capture the strongest opposing case the cap paper has to
survive in round 2.

---

## A. Rory Stewart line of attack

### A1. Show me one country (the precedent challenge)

Every OECD country that had an estate tax with bite has weakened it,
not strengthened it, over the past fifty years:

- Sweden abolished IHT outright in 2004.
- France: progressive erosion of the *droits de succession*; allowances
  raised, business carve-outs widened (Dutreil pact).
- Italy: rates cut, thresholds raised, business succession carve-outs.
- Spain: most regional governments have effectively zero IHT after
  rebates.
- Netherlands: rate cuts and broadened business-succession exemptions.
- US: estate-tax exemption raised from \$675k (2001) to \$13.6M (2024),
  a >\$13M lift in 23 years.

The political economy of inheritance taxation runs in exactly the
opposite direction from the paper's recommendation across democracies
of every flavour. The paper owes the reader an explanation for why
Britain in the 2020s succeeds where every comparable polity has
retreated. There is no such explanation in the current draft.

### A2. The British state cannot implement this

HMRC valuation capacity is already overwhelmed by current IHT
(receipts ~£7B p.a. on a £100T+ household wealth base, suggesting
massive avoidance and undervaluation). The regime exempts agricultural
property relief (APR) and business property relief (BPR) precisely
because every attempt to close those loopholes is destroyed politically
by farmer protests, family-business lobbying, and tax lawyers who
outnumber HMRC operational staff by an order of magnitude.

Look at what happened to Labour's 2024 APR/BPR reform: months of
tractor protests, frantic backpedalling, and the changes do not even
bite until 2027 with a £1M floor. The paper's £5M cap implies an
HMRC capable of valuing privately held companies, art collections,
agricultural land, AIM shares, foreign trusts, and discretionary
settlements at the moment of death, fairly, quickly, and against
well-resourced opposition. That state does not exist in Britain.

### A3. G20 coordination is fantasy

Pillar Two minimum corporate tax took 15 years to agree at 15%, and
the US is currently dismantling it post-2024. The cap paper proposes
G20 coordination on a categorically more invasive instrument that
touches family wealth transfer rather than corporate tax base. The
political economy of this is several orders of magnitude harder than
corporate-tax coordination, which is itself failing.

Anchoring the proposal to G20 coordination is the policy equivalent
of "and then a miracle occurs."

### A4. The bottom-50% baseline is a model artefact

The paper's baseline has the bottom 50% holding 0.0% of household
wealth. The ONS Wealth and Assets Survey puts the actual UK figure
around 9% (housing equity, pensions, durables). Some of that is
genuinely fragile, but it is meaningfully not zero. The headline
effect, going from 0% to 25%, is measured against a model artefact,
not against the real distribution. The honest counterfactual would
be 9% to whatever the policy delivers, with separate accounting of
which wealth components move.

## B. Jeremy Hunt line of attack

### B1. The model has no growth

It is a closed stock-flow accounting system. There is no R&D channel,
no human capital accumulation, no entrepreneurial entry, no
productivity term, no investment-to-capital-stock equation. What the
paper calls "productive flow share" is the share of monetary accruals
to the MAKER agent class. That is bookkeeping, not productivity.

The growth puzzle the UK is actually trying to solve, why UK output
per hour has flatlined since 2008, is invisible in this model. A
policy framework that has nothing to say about productivity cannot
claim to have a verdict on UK fiscal strategy.

### B2. The bottom-50% effect is redistribution, not ownership

Look at the Sankey under the cap. The dominant flow is rentier-to-state
via inheritance excess at death, then state-to-worker via UBI. The
bottom 50% are not owning more capital, they are receiving more
income transfers. This is inheritance-tax-plus-redistribution under
a different name.

If you want the bottom 50% to own assets, you need an asset-building
channel: DC pensions with auto-enrolment, Help to Buy, LISA, Help to
Save, employee ownership trusts. The paper's model has no pension
wrapper, no owner-occupied housing channel, no DC accumulator. The
thing it identifies as ownership is state-mediated income.

### B3. Capital flight is real and rising

The 2024 non-dom reform alone has produced UHNW emigration numbers
that have surprised the Treasury in the wrong direction. CEBR
estimates suggest several billion in lost revenue per year compounding,
plus reduced investment and philanthropic outflows. The paper's
residency-clock and exit-tax wave-of-the-hand is exactly the
wave-of-the-hand the Treasury used in 2022 when modelling the
non-dom change. The Treasury was wrong.

A model with no capital-flight channel cannot be used to recommend
the most flight-sensitive policy on the table.

### B4. £5M captures every UK family business

Median FTSE 250 director shareholding, regional manufacturing firms,
the upper tail of family farms, mid-sized law and accounting
partnerships, anyone who built a business with revenue above ~£20M.
The carve-outs the paper concedes it needs to protect productive
business assets would have to be so wide that the cap mostly does
not bind, in which case the policy reduces to ornamental signalling,
exactly as BPR now does.

Either the cap binds and family businesses break, or it does not
bind and the policy is theatre. The paper does not face this
dilemma squarely.

### B5. The right instrument is the wealth tax, set at a realistic rate

The cap is a maximalist scheme that delivers a one-time stock effect
at enormous political and enforcement cost. The wealth tax is
recurrent, scalable, adjustable, and internationally precedented.
You do not need the cap to do what the cap does, you need a wealth
tax with teeth and an HMRC that can collect it.

## C. The joint counter-paper

If Stewart and Hunt wrote a joint reply, the structure would be:

1. **The model cannot adjudicate this question.** No growth term, no
   flight channel, no enforcement friction, no political-economy
   friction, no real-world ownership wrappers (pensions, housing, DC).
   It is an abstract dynamic of how a closed loop reacts to a parameter
   change. The headline policy claim is not licensed by the model.

2. **The historical record points the other way.** Every comparable
   polity that tried this has retreated. The paper owes the reader an
   explanation for why Britain in the 2020s succeeds where Sweden in
   2004, France in the 2010s, the US across the post-war period, and
   the OECD as a whole have failed. There is no such explanation.

3. **The bottom-50% effect is redistribution under another name.**
   Achievable by IHT-plus-spending, no cap required. The paper has
   not separated the cap-specific contribution from the recirculation
   effect.

4. **Implementation in British institutions is impossible at the
   proposed cap value.** HMRC valuation capacity, BPR/APR politics,
   trust law, and the tax-flight elasticity make £5M unimplementable.
   £25M with carve-outs is the most you could realistically deliver,
   at which point the paper's own sweep shows 86 surviving
   billionaires.

5. **The right ownership policy is bottom-up asset building.**
   DC pension auto-enrolment, LISA expansion, planning reform to
   make housing equity broader, employee ownership trust expansion.
   These are the levers that actually move bottom-half wealth in
   democratic settings.

## D. What the paper would need to add to survive

The strongest version of round 2 would address each line of attack
with a model extension or an empirical robustness check:

| Attack | Required addition |
|---|---|
| A1 precedent | Historical case-study section: why every comparable polity retreated, and the structural conditions that would make the UK 2020s different. Plus a frank concession of the burden of proof. |
| A2 institutional capacity | An enforcement-friction term in the model: probability that wealth above threshold is captured given HMRC valuation noise, BPR/APR carve-outs, trust law. Re-run the policy comparison with realistic enforcement (say, 40-70% capture rate) rather than the implicit 100%. |
| A3 G20 fantasy | Side-by-side: unilateral UK implementation with flight elasticities from Young/Akcigit/Moretti, vs the G20-coordinated counterfactual. Show what the unilateral version delivers, even if it is less. |
| A4 baseline artefact | Recalibrate the baseline so bottom-50% share matches ONS WAS at 9%, not 0%. Re-run the headline effect against the real baseline. |
| B1 no growth | Either (i) add an R&D/productivity term to the model, or (ii) concede explicitly that the paper has nothing to say about growth and frame the contribution as distributional only. |
| B2 redistribution not ownership | Decompose the bottom-50% wealth-share effect into (a) the cap-specific contribution and (b) the spending-package contribution. Run IHT-plus-spending as a separate scenario without the cap, see if the cap adds anything beyond high IHT. |
| B3 capital flight | Add a flight channel: probability of leaving as a function of cap-bite, with elasticities from Young et al. Re-run. |
| B4 family business | Model the BPR/APR-style carve-out explicitly and quantify the policy's bite under realistic carve-outs vs no carve-outs. Show which assets are actually captured. |
| B5 wealth tax superior | Head-to-head: equal-revenue wealth tax vs cap, on bottom-50% share, billionaire count, survival. Find the wealth tax rate that matches the cap's outcomes and report it. |

If even half of these are implemented honestly, the paper's headline
will move from "cap is the structural mechanism for ownership
redistribution" to something more conditional like "under enforcement
assumptions consistent with strong institutional capacity and either
G20 coordination or moderate flight elasticities, an inheritance cap
in the £5-25M range raises bottom-50% wealth share by X percentage
points beyond what an equal-revenue wealth tax achieves." Less
heroic, but credible.

The honest version of this paper survives the Stewart/Hunt attack.
The current version does not.
