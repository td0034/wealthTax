# Why every comparable polity retreated, and what would have to be different

Draft for inclusion in the round 2 cap paper as a new section
positioned after the literature review and before the model summary.
Engages directly with Stewart's "show me one country" critique.

The case for an inheritance cap was made in detail by Mill, Carnegie,
Rignano, and Atkinson. The political economy in every OECD country
that subsequently adopted significant estate taxation has run in the
opposite direction. The honest paper has to explain why.

## The pattern of retreat

In the past 50 years, every comparable polity has weakened, not
strengthened, its inheritance regime.

**Sweden** abolished inheritance tax in 2004 and gift tax in 2005
under a Social Democratic government. The proximate reasons were
the IKEA founding family relocating to Switzerland and persistent
high-profile relocations of business owners.

**France** has progressively narrowed the *droits de succession*.
The Dutreil pact (2003) created a 75% allowance on family-business
shares conditional on a six-year holding commitment. Subsequent
expansions have widened the carve-out.

**Italy** reduced rates and raised thresholds throughout the 2000s.
The current effective rate on lineal descendants above the €1M
threshold is 4%, an order of magnitude lower than the 1990s regime.

**Spain** has nominally aggressive rates but most regional autonomous
governments have applied close-to-100% rebates on lineal descendants.
Effective inheritance taxation in most of Spain is zero.

**Netherlands** cut rates and expanded business-succession exemptions
through the 2010s.

**United States** raised the estate-tax exemption from \$675k in 2001
to \$13.6M in 2024, a 20-fold real-terms increase. The Tax Cuts and
Jobs Act doubled the exemption again in 2017.

**United Kingdom** has held nominal IHT at 40% above £325k since
2009 but effective collection has fallen as a share of the wealth
base. Successive expansions of BPR and APR, the residence nil-rate
band, and the seven-year potentially-exempt-transfer rule have
narrowed the effective base. The 2024 APR/BPR reform attempt provoked
sustained tractor protests and was diluted before implementation.

No major democracy has gone in the opposite direction in the past 50
years. This is not a coincidence and not a conspiracy. It is a
structural pattern that has to be accounted for.

## The five structural reasons

### Reason 1: capital mobility outpaced enforcement capacity

The 1980-2020 period saw progressive liberalisation of capital
controls, the rise of offshore wealth management, the creation of
sophisticated trust structures, and the digitisation of intangible
asset transfer. Estate taxation in 1970 operated on physical assets
in identified jurisdictions. By 2020, the same legal instrument was
attempting to operate on a much more mobile and harder-to-locate
asset base. HMRC's effective enforcement capacity did not scale at
the same rate.

This is the institutional-capacity argument in its empirical form.
The state's tools for estate taxation have weakened in real terms
while the assets to be taxed have become more mobile and more
fungible.

### Reason 2: visible local damage, diffuse benefit

When the IKEA family relocated to Switzerland, the visible
counterfactual was clear: ten thousand Swedish jobs, billions in
local investment, decades of corporate tax revenue, gone. The
benefit of inheritance taxation, by contrast, is diffuse. It accrues
to the median household over a generation in the form of lower
wealth concentration. The political ledger is asymmetric: a single
visible loss vs an invisible distributed gain.

Every country that retreated did so after a visible relocation
shock. Sweden after the IKEA episode. France in response to
Belgian-Luxembourg residency arbitrage. The UK in response to the
post-Brexit non-dom departures.

### Reason 3: the family-business coalition

In every democratic context, a coalition of family-business owners,
farmers, and professional-services partnerships has been the
politically decisive opponent of estate taxation. This coalition is
geographically distributed (which gives it parliamentary leverage
across all constituencies), economically heterogeneous (which makes
it hard to characterise as "the rich"), and culturally legitimate
(family succession, intergenerational stewardship). It is the most
politically effective opposing coalition in the wealth-tax debate.

The 2024 UK APR/BPR reform attempt collapsed under exactly this
coalition. Sweden's 2004 abolition was driven by it. France's
Dutreil pact was its constructive concession.

### Reason 4: the optimal-taxation literature shifted

\citet{piketty2013theory} and \citet{farhi2010progressive} produced
optimal-taxation results that, under common assumptions about
elasticities of bequest and savings response, did not support
high estate taxation as the optimal instrument. The academic
consensus migrated towards capital income tax, consumption tax,
and inheritance taxation only as a third-best instrument with
heavy carve-outs. This consensus shaped Treasury thinking in every
major OECD ministry.

### Reason 5: international coordination has not arrived

The Pillar Two minimum corporate tax framework took 15 years to
negotiate, was diluted to 15%, and is currently being dismantled by
the United States. Estate taxation is a categorically more invasive
instrument because it touches family wealth transfer rather than
corporate tax base. The coordination required to prevent residency
arbitrage on estate taxation is several orders of magnitude harder.
No serious proposal for international coordination on estate taxes
has reached the OECD Inclusive Framework stage.

## What would have to be different for the cap to succeed

The five structural reasons translate into five structural
preconditions for a cap-based proposal to succeed where past
proposals have failed.

1. **Enforcement capacity equal to or exceeding that of corporate
   income tax.** This means digitised real-time asset disclosure,
   beneficial-ownership transparency at the level pioneered by the
   EU AMLD5, automatic exchange of trust information beyond the
   current CRS framework, and HMRC valuation resources comparable to
   the Big Four advisory teams that currently advise estate-planning
   families. The R2-4 enforcement finding suggests this needs to
   exceed 95% effective capture to deliver the model's distributional
   claim.

2. **A pre-committed exit-tax regime with treaty support.** The cap
   must be paired with a residency-clock and an asset-attaching
   provision robust to arbitrage. This is the unilateral-action
   counterpart to the international-coordination requirement.

3. **Class-conditional implementation.** The R2-5 carve-out finding
   suggests the cap should apply to extractive wealth (financial
   holdings, rental property, securities) and exempt productive
   business wealth, with HMRC capacity to classify at death. This
   addresses the family-business coalition by making the cap a
   proposal they have reason to support rather than reason to
   block.

4. **An honest public framing of the trade-off.** The cap is not
   free. It does not raise large revenue. It does not solve the
   growth problem. It is a one-instrument intervention on the
   ownership-distribution problem, and the political case for it
   has to be made on those terms. Selling it as a fiscal-revenue
   instrument has been the strategy that has failed in every
   previous attempt.

5. **G20-level coordination on cap value and exit-tax rules.** The
   precedent here is OECD Pillar Two on corporate minimum tax. The
   precedent shows that coordination is achievable but slow,
   limited in ambition, and vulnerable to subsequent withdrawal. A
   cap proposal that depends on G20 coordination must be honest
   about the timescale and the political vulnerability of any
   coordinated regime.

## What the round 2 paper claims

The honest version of the cap proposal, after engaging with this
precedent record, is:

  *Under enforcement quality, exit-tax provisions, and class-conditional
  carve-outs that no OECD country has demonstrated in the past 50
  years, an inheritance cap in the £5-25M range would deliver
  ownership-distribution redistribution that no wealth tax achieves
  at any rate. The precondition set is severe. The paper does not
  claim that the preconditions are politically achievable in any
  particular jurisdiction. It claims that the structural mechanism
  exists and is identified, that it requires the preconditions
  enumerated, and that the absence of the preconditions in the past
  50 years explains the policy-retreat pattern.*

This is a paper about a structural mechanism whose deployment
requires institutional capacity not yet demonstrated. It is not a
paper about a feasible policy in the current UK fiscal architecture.

That framing is honest, it engages with the precedent record, and
it sets up the right policy question: what institutional capacity
would have to be built to make the cap mechanism deployable. That
is a question on which Fiscal Studies has a constructive answer to
contribute. The current paper does not ask it.
