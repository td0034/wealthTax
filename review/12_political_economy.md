# Political economy of a 5-10% wealth tax above £10M

Strategy memo, not a model run. The model has nothing to say about
political coalitions, parliamentary arithmetic, or media reception.
This memo asks: who champions, who blocks, what are the relevant
precedents, what narrative could land, what the strategic mistakes
to avoid are.

## 1. Why "2%" became the focal point

The Zucman 2% is not derived from any analytical threshold. It is
chosen as the maximum rate that satisfies four political constraints
simultaneously:

1. Visibly above zero (not nominal).
2. Below the perceived "confiscation" line (not zero-sum on the rich).
3. Within the range where revenue projections survive moderate flight
   elasticities (Young et al / Moretti et al).
4. Roughly matching the rate at which UHNW assets currently
   underperform broad indices, so the tax can be framed as
   "yield-capturing" rather than "principal-eroding".

The number is a political construct, not an economic one. It is the
right number for the political function of mobilising cross-
jurisdictional support around a coordinated rate, which is why
Zucman has been so successful at moving the debate.

The empirical problem is that the number does no distributional work
(R2-9, this is the Benhabib threshold). The political problem of
shifting to 5-10% is that it breaks each of the four constraints
above.

## 2. Who could champion 5-10% above £10M

### Domestic (UK)

**Labour left and Green Party MPs.** The natural ideological home.
The 2024 Labour government has not adopted aggressive wealth tax;
the parliamentary left (McDonnell, Burgon, Sultana, Green coalition)
has consistently called for instruments stronger than the current
IHT regime. A 5-10% wealth tax above £10M is closer to their
existing rhetoric than Zucman 2%.

The risk: identification with the parliamentary left makes the
proposal easier to dismiss as fringe-left rather than evidence-based.
The Fiscal Studies framing helps, but only marginally.

**The Institute for Fiscal Studies / Resolution Foundation / Tax
Justice Network.** Think-tank infrastructure exists. RF has published
on wealth taxes since 2020; the Wealth Tax Commission (2020-21,
Loutzenhiser, Summers, Advani) is the most serious recent academic
treatment. Their recommended one-off wealth tax was 5% above £500k,
spread over five years. Our proposal is structurally different (a
continuous rate) but the rate-tier numbers are not as far apart as
they look (5% per year for 5 years = 25% on the asset; their one-off
5% spread over 5 years is 5% total). We are within an order of
magnitude of the most recent serious UK academic proposal.

**Patriotic Millionaires UK.** A small but real signatory pool of
UHNWs publicly advocating taxation on themselves. The proposal as
"the level wealth tax that actually does distributional work" might
land here, where Zucman 2% lands as "modest enough that we can sign
without consequence".

### International

**Norwegian and Spanish finance ministries.** Both have operational
wealth-tax infrastructure. Both have raised rates in 2022-23.
Coordination with these countries on a higher G20 minimum rate is
the most plausible international path. The Norwegian wealth-tax
debate is already at 1.5% top rate in serious discussion; our
proposal is the empirically derived target of that debate.

**The EU Tax Observatory (Zucman's home institution).** They would
not endorse 10% as written because it breaks their political model
of the 2% G20 coordination strategy. They might endorse the
analytical observation that 2% is below the threshold, and engage
with the question of what rate the threshold actually is. The
analytical engagement is the right starting move; the policy
disagreement comes downstream.

## 3. Who opposes and how strongly

### Family-business coalition

The coalition that destroyed the 2024 UK APR/BPR reform attempt and
historically every comparable estate-tax reform.

For the hybrid wealth tax this coalition is weaker than for the cap
because:
1. The tax is paid from flow not stock; no forced sale.
2. The threshold is £10M, well above median family-business value.
3. The continuous nature means small failures of cash flow trigger
   ordinary tax-debt remedies, not estate-sale crises.

For the hybrid wealth tax this coalition is stronger than for
Zucman 2% because:
1. 10% per year is large enough to bite on businesses with
   £10M-£50M valuations.
2. The CBI, IoD, FSB will mobilise on growth-and-investment grounds.
3. Owner-managed business sector media (FT Wealth, City AM, Money
   Marketing) will frame as "British Capital-eating Itself".

The honest assessment: the coalition is more manageable than for the
cap but still real. The 5% rate is more politically defensible
against this coalition than the 10%.

### Treasury orthodoxy

HM Treasury's standard position is anti-wealth-tax on three grounds:
1. Administrative cost / valuation complexity.
2. Tax-flight elasticities.
3. The "Mirrlees Review" academic consensus
   \citep{mirrlees2011tax} that capital income tax is the right
   instrument and wealth tax adds nothing.

The first ground is weakened by the existing Norwegian / Swiss /
Spanish operational templates. The second is partially answerable
with the R2-11A flight robustness finding. The third requires
engaging the Mirrlees framework directly: their argument is
optimal-tax-theoretic and assumes a particular social welfare
function and bequest motive structure; our paper is mechanism-
identifying and does not depend on those assumptions.

### Centre-right and Conservative MPs

Predictable opposition framed as growth, competitiveness, capital
mobility. The Conservative party is committed (post-Truss) to
fiscal stability rather than aggressive tax cuts, but adopting a
5-10% wealth tax is in no version of that strategy.

The strategic question is whether there is a constructive Tory
position. The honest answer is probably not at the 10% rate. At
the 5% rate the proposal can be defended on Carnegie-Mill grounds
(philosophical conservatism about dynastic privilege) but the
modern Conservative party has no Carnegie-Mill faction.

## 4. ISF failure mode (France 1981-2017)

The French \emph{Impôt sur la fortune} (later Impôt de solidarité
sur la fortune) operated from 1981 to 2017, with rates topping at
1.5% above €10M. It was abolished by Macron in 2017 in favour of
an asset-based tax restricted to real estate.

Why ISF failed:
1. **Coverage was too broad.** The threshold was €1.3M, capturing
   middle-class Parisian property owners. Political opposition was
   geographically distributed.
2. **The rate was below the capital return.** Same Benhabib problem.
   ISF raised €4-5bn per year but did not change the top
   distribution. So it was politically costly without being
   distributionally effective: the worst of both worlds.
3. **Capital flight was real and visible.** Estimates suggest 10,000
   millionaires left France 2000-2017; documented in
   \citet{durand2020fiscal} and Bessard \& Marques.
4. **No equivalent instrument abroad.** Other major economies did
   not have ISF, making relocation easy.

Implications for our proposal:
- Threshold should be high (£10M). ISF's £1M-equivalent was the
  political error.
- Rate should be above r. ISF's 1.5% was below capital return.
- Coordination is essential. ISF's unilateral nature was the
  flight mechanism.

The ISF failure is therefore consistent with our analytical reading.
ISF tried the politically maximum sustainable rate (1.5%) at a low
threshold (€1.3M); we propose the analytically required rate (5-10%)
at a high threshold (£10M). Different parameters, different
political profile.

## 5. Norway template

Norway operates a wealth tax continuously, with the top rate at
1.1% above NOK 1.7M (~£130k). The 2022 reform raised the top rate
0.15pp and prompted documented UHNW relocations to Switzerland.

What works in Norway:
1. **Operational infrastructure.** Annual asset valuation through
   Skatteetaten, beneficial-ownership transparency via the
   beneficial-owner register.
2. **Cultural legitimacy.** The wealth tax is politically embedded
   across centre-left and centre-right parties.
3. **Modest rate.** At 1.1% the tax is below r=8% but functions as
   a revenue instrument; the analytical limit is not relevant
   because the political function is revenue, not redistribution.

What fails: at 1.1% the wealth tax does not deliver structural
redistribution; the top distribution in Norway tracks the same
upward trajectory as in the UK and US over 2000-2024.

Implications: the Norwegian model is the right operational
template. The Norwegian rate is too low for the structural function
we identify. The honest pitch is "operate the Norwegian
infrastructure at the higher rate the analytical work requires".

## 6. Spain solidarity tax (2022-present)

Spain introduced a temporary wealth tax in 2022 (renewed 2024)
with rates up to 3.5% above €10M. The base is narrow (UHNW only)
and the tax is paid in addition to existing regional wealth taxes
in some communities (Catalonia, Asturias).

What this shows: the political ceiling on European wealth tax rates
has moved upward since 2020. The Spanish 3.5% rate is the highest
sustained rate currently operating in a G20 country. Our proposal
at 5-10% is 1.4-2.8x the highest current operational rate, which
is a marginal step rather than a categorical one if framed against
Spain.

The Spanish tax has not yet produced documented flight data
(too recent). The Catalan regional wealth tax (operational since
2011 at rates up to 2.75%) has produced limited but documented
millionaire relocations to Madrid (zero wealth tax under regional
autonomy). This is the within-EU flight channel that constrains
any unilateral UK action.

## 7. Strategic recommendations

### Anchor the proposal to 5%, not 10%

The empirical sweep shows 5% above £10M matches the cap on
bottom-50% lift and eliminates billionaires. 10% delivers more
(42% vs 19% bot-50) but the political cost rises faster than the
distributional benefit. The 5% rate is also 4-5x Norway (the
current EU template) rather than 9x; the comparator is more
defensible.

The headline number for the public-facing proposal should be:

  **5% per year on wealth above £10M, phased in over 5 years
  from a starting 1% rate, applied universally with the existing
  IHT regime continuing alongside.**

The 10% rate is the empirical dominance result. The 5% rate is the
political proposal. Both should be in the paper; only the 5% should
be in the policy summary and any communication piece.

### Frame as "the rate the Zucman framework actually requires"

The Zucman 2% proposal is the politically established focal point.
Attacking it directly is the wrong move; the right move is to
position 5-10% as the empirically derived rate within the Zucman
G20 framework, with the analytical Benhabib threshold as the
justification.

This is consistent with the paper's framing. The framing should be
extended in any communication: the 2% rate is the right framework
on threshold, coordination, and base; only the rate level is
empirically too low.

### Avoid the ISF mistakes

Three explicit no-go zones:
1. Do not propose threshold below £5M.
2. Do not propose rates below r=8% (4-5% above £10M is the floor;
   below 5% reproduces the ISF failure mode).
3. Do not propose unilateral UK implementation without exit-tax
   and residency-clock provisions.

### Sequencing

The communication piece should sequence:

1. Lead with the Benhabib analytical observation. The 2% number is
   below the threshold. This is the empirical-analytical opening.
2. Show the empirical sweep. At 5% the wealth tax matches the cap;
   at 10% it dominates.
3. Show the enforcement robustness. The cap collapses; the hybrid
   does not.
4. Frame the policy at 5%, with 10% as the analytical dominance
   rate.
5. Position as Zucman+ rather than Zucman-replace.

### Who to approach first

If the goal is academic-policy impact:
1. Resolution Foundation (Mike Brewer, Greg Thwaites).
2. Tax Justice Network UK (Robert Palmer).
3. Wealth Tax Commission authors (Loutzenhiser, Summers, Advani).
4. Patriotic Millionaires UK (Phil White).

If the goal is parliamentary engagement:
5. Backbench Treasury Select Committee members.
6. Green Party tax spokesperson.

If the goal is international coordination:
7. EU Tax Observatory (Zucman's group) for analytical engagement.
8. Norwegian Ministry of Finance for operational template
   discussion.

### The mistake to avoid

The deepest strategic mistake would be to present the paper as
"a wealth tax 5x bigger than Zucman". This invites immediate
dismissal as fringe. The right framing is "the analytical work
shows the Zucman framework is on the right track and the rate
needs to be at the empirically derived level required for the
mechanism to deliver".

This is honest, it is what the paper actually shows, and it is
politically constructive rather than confrontational.
